# AMERICAN INJUSTICE
### A Manuscript Draft — Full Chronological Narrative
*Compiled from sworn court filings, contemporaneous recordings, and Matthew Reardon's own account. Sourcing key: [CONFIRMED] = independently corroborated by a court finding, a second party's own words on tape, or an official document. [RECORDED] = Reardon's own account, captured on tape at the time, but not independently corroborated by a third party or document. [CLAIM] = asserted by Reardon, no corroboration located yet — flagged for fact-check before publication.*

---

## AUTHOR'S NOTE ON METHOD

Every scene in this book is built from a primary source: a phone recording, a sworn affidavit, a judge's written order, a police dispatch log. Where Matthew Reardon's account and the documentary record agree, that agreement is stated plainly. Where they diverge, or where only his voice exists, that is stated plainly too. This is not a legal brief arguing his innocence, and it is not a takedown of the officials who crossed his path. It is an attempt to lay out, in order, what actually happened — and to let the reader watch a nine-year pattern take shape one recorded phone call at a time.

The throughline that survives contact with the evidence is this: a documented pattern of retaliatory conduct by specific law-enforcement and court officials in Lafayette County, Mississippi, running alongside a genuine, escalating estrangement between Reardon and the institutions meant to hear him out — an estrangement his own rhetoric helped produce. Both things are true. This book tries to hold both at once.

## PROLOGUE — THE CALL YOU'VE HEARD BEFORE

If you have spent any time around Matthew Reardon, you have heard this story. You have heard it in a truck cab driving to Justice Court, in a voicemail to a United States Senator's office, in a phone call to a small-town newspaper editor, in a jailhouse call placed collect at government expense. The details do not shift. The names do not change. Sheriff Joey East. Judge John Kelly Luther. An AR-15 bought before anyone says it was bought. A sallyport video that was never turned over because — by the Sheriff's own sworn admission — no one preserved it.

That consistency is the case Reardon has always made for himself: I have told it the same way every time, to everyone, for nine years, because it is what happened.

This book does not take that consistency as proof on its own. Consistency is necessary but not sufficient — a person can repeat a distorted account with total sincerity for a decade. What makes this book possible is that enough of the account can now be checked against something outside Reardon's own memory: a federal magistrate's finding that a police officer lied under oath; a sheriff's own sworn testimony that evidence was not preserved; a receptionist's real-time, unprompted explanation of how judges get reassigned in Mississippi's court system, sitting on tape directly beside Reardon's own account of a conspiracy. Where the record checks out, this book says so. Where it doesn't — or where only Reardon's voice exists — this book says that too.

## CHAPTER ONE — THE SETUP (2017)

By the spring of 2017, Matthew Reardon had already made himself a known quantity in Oxford, Mississippi — a Marine veteran, a small-business owner, and increasingly, a public irritant to the local political establishment. On June 6, 2017, Reardon intended to speak at an Oxford City Hall meeting about what he believed was an embezzlement matter involving Ray Tannehill, husband of Mayor Robin Tannehill — a man who would later, not coincidentally, become his ex-wife's custody attorney.

He never got the chance to speak that day. Twelve days earlier, on May 26, 2017, Reardon was arrested in what he has described consistently, from the very first telling, as a coordinated ambush: four officers — Chief Deputy Scott Mills, Major Alan Wilburn, Lieutenant Jared Bundren, and Sheriff Joey East himself — converging on him at once. [RECORDED — Reardon's consistent account across multiple calls this decade]

The arrest came two days after a call to Bundren on May 24, 2017 that Reardon has long described as a "mystery call" — someone reaching out, refusing to say why. In a 2026 recorded conversation with his late friend and attorney Andy Arant, Reardon connects new dots: "A couple days before the 26th, Joey East was trying to get in touch with me. He was calling me. He won't tell me what it was about. But then on the 26th, the day I was ambushed, he sent an email trying to change the gun carry laws in Oxford... I have the email... now it makes sense that that mystery phone call to Jared Bundren on the 24th, the person that it actually makes the most sense to being now is Joey East." [RECORDED — Reardon to Andy Arant, num2349910_b] Reardon says a physical email exists proving Sheriff East pushed to change Oxford's gun-carry ordinance on the very day of the arrest — the day after Reardon had legally carried an AR-15 in public. That email has not yet been independently located and read by this author; it is the single most important documentary lead in the entire early chronology, and its recovery — confirmed by Don Reardon's own more recent statement that such emails do exist in his Google Drive — is a priority before this book goes to print. [CLAIM, pending document recovery]

Held for 96 hours — twice the 48-hour statutory maximum for a warrantless hold — Reardon was eventually presented with a plea deal carrying a five-year banishment from Lafayette County and a covenant not to sue, in exchange for a guilty plea to aggravated stalking. [RECORDED, corroborated in part by the reconstructed chronology below] Bond was set at $150,000 — a figure his attorney Andy Arant would later call, on tape, higher than typical bonds set in the same jurisdiction for rape or murder charges.

It is Arant's own recorded words, not Reardon's, that make the legal case for what happened next: "if that's not duress, I don't know what is." Arant, a Water Valley public defender who mentored Reardon for years before his death from cancer, laid out on tape the actual, focused legal argument for challenging the plea — not the sprawling due-process and motive theories Reardon wanted to argue, but the narrow, provable one: no bond-reduction hearing, no preliminary hearing, six weeks in jail before a plea was offered under a bond nearly triple the norm. [CONFIRMED — Arant's own words on tape, num2349910_b]

Attorney Kristian McRay filed a habeas petition on Reardon's behalf on June 5, 2017, approved for in forma pauperis status the next day. Reardon says he was never brought before a judge and only learned of the habeas filing after his release on July 6, 2017. [RECORDED] Reardon has long maintained he can prove, through a combination of recorded jail calls and a witness he calls "Larry" — the seller of the AR-15 — that the gun's actual purchase date undercuts the timeline of the charge against him; as of this writing, the bill of sale itself has not been located. [CLAIM, corroboration pending — see Chapter Fourteen, Reconciling the Record]

Arant told Reardon plainly, on tape, that no local attorney wanted to take on the Sheriff's Office "unless they've got a slam dunk case" — and separately predicted that Chancellor Lawrence Little would eventually recuse himself from Reardon's matters after reading a lengthy letter Reardon had sent him describing the alleged corruption. [RECORDED] Little would, years later, become the judge who signed the writ committing Reardon to a mental health facility in December 2021 — a fact this book returns to in Chapter Six.

## CHAPTER TWO — THE ACCUSATION (2018)

In November 2018, Reardon's ex-wife, Phyllis Liz Crowder (later Kester), accused him of rape. Reardon has said, and a recorded call appears to confirm, that she later admitted — on tape, in conversation with her then-husband — to conspiring with him to fabricate the allegation. Olive Branch Police Department ultimately charged her; she served no jail time. [RECORDED] When Reardon attempted to raise the false accusation in a subsequent proceeding, the presiding judge held him in contempt for doing so. [RECORDED]

That same period saw Detective Holliday of the Olive Branch Police Department execute a consent-to-search and, separately, a search warrant, seizing Reardon's phone in connection with text messages to Crowder. Reardon has alleged the warrant was obtained after a scope-limited consent had already been signed — a sequencing question, if accurate, that would matter considerably to any Fourth Amendment claim, but one this book has not yet independently verified against the actual warrant application and its timestamp. [RECORDED, sequencing unverified]

## CHAPTER THREE — A PATTERN OF STOPS (2020–2021)

On December 28, 2020, Deputies Courtney Dixon and Ethan Tidwell arrested Reardon at the county Sallyport. Reardon describes being "beaten." Tidwell's own sworn report describes a takedown-to-cuff, not a beating, and no dash or body camera footage exists to adjudicate between the two descriptions. [RECORDED — Reardon's account; CONFIRMED — Tidwell's contradicting sworn account exists] This book presents both descriptions rather than adopting either.

On February 21, 2021, Deputy Wiliford and Deputy Beavers conducted a traffic stop at the same Sallyport, citing the odor of marijuana. Wiliford's own report describes no signs of impairment. No video exists. [RECORDED, Wiliford's report itself does not allege impairment] Reardon has long pointed to the recurring use of this specific location — a sallyport, ordinarily a secure vehicle-transfer point at a jail, not a public roadway — as evidence of a pattern of stops conducted without a documented, ordinary basis. That the Sheriff's own sworn admission (Chapter Six) confirms a sallyport video from a separate, earlier incident was never preserved lends this pattern real weight, even though no video exists from either the Dixon/Tidwell or Wiliford/Beavers incidents specifically to examine directly.

That September, a records request Reardon submitted regarding drone surveillance was met with a stonewall he has described consistently since. [RECORDED, document trail not yet independently reconstructed]

## CHAPTER FOUR — GOING OVER THEIR HEADS (AUGUST 2021)

This is the chapter that changes the shape of the whole book, because it moves the documentary record four months earlier than anyone previously understood.

On August 2, 2021, Reardon left a voicemail with U.S. Representative Matt Gaetz's Florida office. On the recording, he makes a claim not found anywhere else in the archive: that the FBI met with him the day before his May 26, 2017 arrest, and that the report of that meeting was later destroyed. [RECORDED — Reardon's voicemail, uncorroborated by any FBI record found to date; this is a distinct, serious claim that requires its own fact-check pass before publication] On the same call, he offers a separate, checkable claim: that he was "locked up for six weeks... from a week before she was elected until a week after she was sworn into office" — an apparent reference to an election-timing detail that does not obviously reconcile with the documented 96-hour hold from the May 2017 arrest, and needs review against the full jail and court record before it appears in the final text. [CLAIM, requires reconciliation]

Eleven days later, on August 13, 2021, and again on August 23, 2021, Reardon called the DC office of U.S. Senator Cindy Hyde-Smith. Staff on the second call told him plainly: "she's not going to be able to do much... Governor Reeves would probably be your best bet." [CONFIRMED — staff's own words, recorded] It is what Reardon says next that matters most to the book's larger argument. Months before he says he discovered the judge-reassignment irregularity in his own case files that December, he articulates, in full, to a U.S. Senator's staff, the theory that would later anchor his federal complaint: "the judge that is actually picking the judges... is the same circuit court judge I feel is criminally liable." [RECORDED]

This detail cuts two ways, and this book will not pretend otherwise. It can be read as evidence that Reardon was onto something real well before he had documentary proof of it — a whistleblower whose instincts outran his paperwork. It can equally be read as evidence that Reardon had already settled on a theory of persecution before the specific facts he would later cite as its proof had occurred, and simply slotted the December discovery into a frame he'd built months earlier. Chapter Fourteen returns to this tension directly; the honest answer is that the recordings alone cannot resolve it.

## CHAPTER FIVE — THE REASSIGNMENT (DECEMBER 2021)

On December 3, 2021, Board Attorney David O'Donnell sent an email — later recited in the court's own factual findings in Reardon's federal suit — warning county clerks that Reardon was "verbally abusive" and that his contact was "increasing in frequency and intensity." [CONFIRMED — recited in the court's own order in Reardon v. Lafayette Cnty., 3:22-CV-50-SA-JMV]

Three days later, on December 6, 2021, something the court's own order independently describes as a "mysterious altering of court records regarding assignment of judge" occurred. Circuit Clerk Jeff Busby grew defensive and placed a call to someone off-camera. Sheriff East physically blocked Reardon's car and ordered him not to return to the Circuit Court "unless he has something to file." [CONFIRMED — the court's own recitation of the pled facts describes this event; East's blocking of the car and the order not to return are Reardon's account of that same day, RECORDED]

This is the episode Reardon had, without yet knowing its specifics, already previewed to a U.S. Senator's staff four months earlier.

It connects directly to Reardon's fullest, most detailed account of the underlying allegation — a recorded 2026 call to a woman named "Denise" at a number identified as 662-638-3093. On tape, Reardon says: "Judge John Kelly Luther, the circuit court judge... he was the original judge back in 2017... there was a fraudulent changing of judges in the circuit court system, my post conviction relief was originally assigned to Judge Kent Smith and Judge John Kelly Luther kept going over and denying my motions... he's picking the judges for justice court... it's pointing back to him, because he had knowledge [of crimes committed by Lafayette County officials]." [RECORDED]

What happens next on that same call is, in this author's judgment, the single most important piece of tape in the entire archive — not because it proves or disproves the conspiracy, but because it captures, unscripted and in real time, the strongest good-faith counter-explanation anyone offers anywhere in this record. Denise responds: "every Lafayette County justice court judge has recused himself and because that has happened, it is up to the circuit court judge to have superior jurisdiction over the justice court judge and Judge Luther is the senior circuit court judge, so as his role as senior circuit court judge, it's his duty to find a judge to replace all the judges in the justice court that say there's a conflict and have been recused. That's so common across the state." [RECORDED]

A necessary correction belongs here. A later 2026 recording identifies a "Denise Fondren" as Reardon's own court-appointed defense attorney in Justice Court — a woman Reardon separately alleges was affiliated with the Earlene Gardner Victim Assistance Project, a nonprofit whose mission, he argues, put her in conflict with representing him against his ex-wife's interests. [RECORDED — Reardon's allegation; CONFIRMED, per Reardon, that Denise Fondren served as his court-appointed counsel] Whether the "Denise" on the judge-shopping call is this same Denise Fondren, or a different Denise entirely — perhaps a genuinely neutral clerk — has not yet been resolved, and it matters. If she is his own attorney, her explanation carries less weight as disinterested institutional testimony and more as a lawyer managing an anxious client. If she is a separate, uninvolved clerk, her account stands as the closest thing in this book to a neutral, on-the-record rebuttal. This distinction must be resolved — through phone metadata, a caller-ID cross-reference, or a direct records request — before this exchange is published as written. [OPEN ITEM — highest-priority fact-check remaining in the manuscript]

## CHAPTER SIX — COMMITTED (DECEMBER 2021 – FEBRUARY 2022)

On December 9, 2021, Chancellor Lawrence Little — the same judge Andy Arant had predicted, years earlier, would eventually recuse himself after reading Reardon's letter — signed a writ committing Reardon to the Tupelo Crisis Center. The commitment ran through December 23, 2021. The underlying affidavit was filed by Rachel Alcorn, a clinical director at Communicare, who identified herself as a "relative and/or interested person, residing at Communicare," and cited a "recent attempt/threat to harm" and a "failure/inability to provide necessary medical care." [CONFIRMED — recited in the court's own order]

A second commitment followed on February 9, 2022, this one on an affidavit filed by Susan Beard, alleging Reardon had missed a scheduled appointment. [CONFIRMED — recited in the court's own order] This commitment ran approximately 30 days.

Alcorn's own explanation — not adopted here as the final word, but recorded for balance — is that a pre-existing outpatient commitment order from Calhoun County was in effect, and that Reardon had a documented history of non-compliance with it. [Alcorn's stated position, as reflected in the court record] The timing of both commitments, arriving within weeks of the December 6 court-records incident and the federal complaint that followed, remains suspicious on its face. But "suspicious timing" and "a real, pre-existing legal mechanism being invoked" are not mutually exclusive, and this book will not claim they are.

It was in this period that Reardon filed his own twelve-page "Affidavit/Declaration of Truth" against Sheriff East — a document written in a style common to sovereign-citizen legal filings. When a court official later told Reardon directly, on the record, that he was "acting like a sovereign citizen," Reardon's own recorded response was candid: it "feels that way at times." [RECORDED] This is the clearest moment in the entire archive where Reardon, in his own voice, acknowledges the perception problem his own filings created — a perception problem that this book argues did real damage to his credibility with the very institutions he was trying to reach, independent of whether his underlying claims had merit.

## CHAPTER SEVEN — THE FEDERAL SUIT (APRIL 2022)

On April 6, 2022, Reardon filed a pro se federal lawsuit — later amended July 19, 2022 — seeking roughly $27.5 million in damages. Named defendants included the State of Mississippi, Lafayette County, the Lafayette County Chancery Court, Communicare, the Mississippi Department of Mental Health, the Lafayette County Sheriff's Department, Sheriff Joseph B. East, Dr. Sandy Rogers, Rachel Alcorn, Susan Beard, Chancery Clerk Sherry Wall, Circuit Clerk Jeff Busby, and Board Attorney David O'Donnell. Seventeen separate claims were pled.

The State of Mississippi and its Department of Mental Health were dismissed on Eleventh Amendment sovereign-immunity grounds without prejudice. On June 28, 2023, Judge Sharion Aycock dismissed every remaining claim with prejudice, closing the case.

This is a fact this book states plainly and without hedging: Reardon lost this case, completely, and the loss was final. What the court's own written order does independently confirm, in its recitation of the facts as pled, is the underlying timeline above — the O'Donnell email, the December 6 records irregularity, the two commitments, the named officials. The order corroborates that these events happened substantially as Reardon described them at the time. It does not corroborate that they amounted to a legally actionable conspiracy, and the court explicitly held they did not.

Reardon has framed the suit's outcome as a moral victory regardless — noting that defendants collectively spent six figures answering a 74-page, pro se, in forma pauperis complaint. [RECORDED, Reardon's own framing] This book presents that framing as his interpretation, not as a finding of the court.

## CHAPTER NINE — THE CANDID CALL (OCTOBER 3, 2022)

Three days after the plea, McClinton called Reardon. This recording — 221003_1800.m4a — is among the most consequential in the entire archive, because it is the one place where an attorney, not Reardon, volunteers corroboration of the surveillance narrative unprompted: "they knew where you were today... they're following you, dedicating resources to you." [RECORDED — McClinton's own words]

On the same call, a specific, checkable coincidence surfaces: Reardon's 2017 case had a ten-day extension window pending at the Mississippi Court of Appeals, and Reardon says he was arrested on the new felony during that exact window. [CLAIM, requires direct docket verification — see Chapter Fourteen]

McClinton also describes a conversation with Assistant U.S. Attorney Bob Norman, who initially indicated he would refer the matter to the DOJ's Civil Rights Division, then reportedly backed off once he learned Reardon intended to also pursue a federal civil suit. McClinton, notably, calls Norman "about the most stand-up prosecutor" he knows — a characterization this book preserves specifically because it complicates a simple villain narrative around Norman's eventual non-referral. [RECORDED, both the walk-back and McClinton's positive characterization of Norman]

This same call introduces a distinct judicial-conduct thread, separate from Chancellor Little: Chancery Judge Whitwell. While Reardon was incarcerated following the June 2022 recharge, Whitwell dismissed his custody matter with prejudice, cut off his contact with one daughter, and set child support at roughly $400–600 per month. Reardon filed a writ of mandamus and prohibition with the Mississippi Supreme Court. In a detail that stands on its own without embellishment, Whitwell himself denied Reardon's pauper's-oath petition on the very claim brought against Whitwell. McClinton's assessment on tape: "you have a fucking case there." [RECORDED — CONFIRMED as to the sequence of the pauper's-oath denial, per court record referenced in the call] The Mississippi Supreme Court likewise denied the pauper's-oath request, offering an unaffordable filing window given Reardon's incarceration — closing the appeal procedurally rather than on the merits.

McClinton's advice was pragmatic and, in its own way, telling: don't pursue Chancellor Little, who is well-connected; pursue Whitwell instead. [RECORDED] McClinton's own background is relevant context for his credibility as a source, not as a fact about Reardon's case: a former Assistant Attorney General under Jim Hood, McClinton alleges his own wife was fired from a state position after the 2019 election specifically because of their shared last name — a separate, parallel claim of institutional retaliation that this book keeps clearly distinct from Reardon's own narrative. [McClinton's own claim about his own life, not independently verified by this author, and not treated as corroboration of Reardon's claims]

By this point Reardon's income had fallen from roughly $10,000 a month in 2017 to $500–600 a month.

## CHAPTER ELEVEN — GALVESTON (AUGUST 2023)

In August 2023, Reardon was stopped by Officer William Osteen of the Galveston, Texas Police Department. The forty-six recordings recovered from the Galveston Police Department's own dispatch archive tell a granular story that this book can reconstruct almost minute by minute.

At approximately 2:05 AM, the stop began. At 2:10 AM, a bystander confirmed on a recorded call that Reardon had willingly offered a breathalyzer test — directly undercutting any later suggestion of refusal. [CONFIRMED — third-party bystander, recorded] At 2:19 AM, Galveston PD warned hospital security that "an auditor" was en route. At 2:41–2:48 AM, Sergeant Hoby James called in after watching a YouTube livestream of the encounter. At 3:03–3:10 AM, a caller identifying himself as "James Kane" requested a welfare check; dispatcher Julie confirmed on tape that Osteen was present with Reardon at the hospital, and mocked him as "one of the auditors." [CONFIRMED — dispatcher's own recorded words]

Between 3:44 and 4:04 AM, Mississippi dispatch — first an officer named James, then Beavers — was unable to confirm Reardon's conviction status beyond an ambiguous reference to "cycle 6 inmate intake, no charge affiliated." Nowhere on any of the forty-six recordings does anyone from Mississippi affirmatively state that Reardon had a "final conviction, no pending appeal." [CONFIRMED, by the absence of any such statement across the full recovered dispatch archive] That specific conclusion — the one used to escalate the charge against Reardon — was constructed by Galveston Police Department and Osteen themselves, not relayed to them by Mississippi authorities. This is not this book's inference; it is the documented absence of the claim anywhere on tape, set against the department's own subsequent actions.

The federal magistrate's Report and Recommendation in Reardon v. Osteen (3:25-cv-00203, S.D. Tex.) later found something remarkable in its own right: Osteen swore in his warrant affidavit that he smelled alcohol on Reardon, and the recorded evidence contradicts that sworn statement. The magistrate found the resulting tow was an unreasonable seizure, and — separately and explicitly — found that Osteen lied under oath. [CONFIRMED — federal magistrate's own written finding] Qualified immunity was nonetheless granted on the underlying seizure claim, a legal outcome distinct from, and not erasing, the court's own finding about Osteen's sworn dishonesty.

## CHAPTER FOURTEEN — RECONCILING THE RECORD

Several threads in this book remain open, and this chapter exists to say so plainly rather than let them quietly disappear into the narrative.

First, the exact legal nexus between the September 30, 2022 guilty plea and the July 2024 two-year sentence has not yet been established from primary documents — only from Reardon's own account that the sentence followed "a further violation." The Mississippi Court of Appeals opinion in Reardon v. State, and the relevant Oxford Eagle articles from November 2022 and July 2024, need to be read directly before this book states the nexus as fact.

Second, the coincidence McClinton raises on tape — a ten-day rehearing-extension window at the Court of Appeals overlapping with the June 28, 2022 recharge — needs to be checked against the actual appellate docket dates before it can be presented as more than a striking coincidence Reardon's own attorney found notable in the moment.

Third, the claim that Reardon was jailed "six weeks... from a week before she was elected until a week after she was sworn into office" (Gaetz voicemail, August 2021) needs reconciliation against the documented 96-hour hold from the May 2017 arrest. These may describe two different periods of incarceration entirely; conflating them would be an error this book must not make.

Fourth, and most urgently: the identity question at the center of Chapter Five — whether "Denise" and "Denise Fondren" are the same person — needs to be resolved through something firmer than tone of voice on a recording. This single fact determines whether the archive's best moment of institutional balance is a neutral clerk's explanation or a conflicted attorney's defense of her own office's process.

Fifth: the FBI-report-destruction claim from the Gaetz voicemail (Chapter Four) is a serious, specific allegation — that a federal agency destroyed a report of a meeting with Reardon the day before his 2017 arrest — and it currently rests entirely on Reardon's own recorded assertion. It needs its own dedicated fact-check pass, including a FOIA request to the FBI if one has not already been filed, before this book can present it as anything more than a claim.

Sixth: the physical email Reardon says exists, showing Sheriff East's push to change Oxford's gun-carry ordinance on the day of the May 2017 arrest, needs to be located and read in full. Reardon has confirmed such records exist in his Google Drive; retrieving them is the single highest-value remaining task before this manuscript can be considered complete, because it would convert the book's founding claim from an assertion into a document.

## EPILOGUE — THE DUALITY

Read end to end, the record assembled here supports something more complicated than a single villain or a single lie. It spans a county sheriff's department, a chancery court, a circuit court, a state mental-health contractor, a Texas municipal police department, and, eventually, federal prosecutors — several of whom, independently of one another, left behind evidence of real misconduct: a sheriff's sworn admission that evidence was not preserved, a Texas officer's sworn statement a federal magistrate found to be a lie, testimony at a revocation hearing contradicted by the record, a warning email about a citizen sent quietly to court clerks.

But the record also documents, in Reardon's own words and choices, an escalating rhetorical style — filings in the language of sovereign-citizen legal theory, a national-security framing offered to congressional staff months before the facts that would later justify it had occurred — that gave every institution he approached an easy, non-conspiratorial reason to stop listening. Somewhere in the space between a citizen telling the truth and a citizen sounding too extreme to be believed, both things can be happening to the same person, in the same decade, and a system built to sort the credible from the incredible can fail at exactly that sorting task, over and over, for years.

That failure — not a single sheriff, not a single judge — is the American injustice this book is named for.
