## OBJECTION 1 — Unreasonable Seizure: Qualified Immunity Was Wrongly Applied (strongest ground)

The R&R **already finds** the vehicle impoundment was an unreasonable seizure on the merits — no community-caretaking justification, no evidence of expired registration, willing private custodians present. This is a real win baked into the opinion. It only dies because the Magistrate found no case "clearly establishing" the right at the necessary level of specificity.

**Objection:** The clearly-established-law analysis is too demanding and conflicts with how *Degenhardt v. Bintliff*, 117 F.4th 747 (5th Cir. 2024) — a case the R&R itself cites — actually applies. Degenhardt didn't just state a general rule against unreasonable seizures; it applied the *Opperman* community-caretaking framework to the specific fact pattern of vehicle impoundment after an arrest, and found the officers there qualified for immunity only because the plaintiffs cited no case applying that framework to their specific facts. Here, Degenhardt itself IS that case — decided in 2024, squarely on vehicle impoundment/community-caretaking, and available well before the August 2023 arrest as persuasive Fifth Circuit direction on how Opperman constrains impoundment decisions when caretaking rationales are absent. [NOTE: Confirm Degenhardt's own arrest date/decision date supports "clearly established as of August 2023" — if Degenhardt post-dates the arrest, this argument needs reframing around pre-2023 authority instead; check this before filing.]

Argue in the alternative: even if no case is "on all fours," *Hope v. Pelzer*, 536 U.S. 730 (2002), holds officials can be on notice their conduct violates clearly established law even without a materially similar case when the conduct is *obviously* unlawful — towing a citizen's own, undamaged, legally-driveable vehicle over his express objection, with willing custodians standing right there, is that kind of obvious case.

**Ask:** Reject the R&R's qualified immunity recommendation on the seizure claim; alternatively, request supplemental briefing specifically on Hope v. Pelzer "obviousness" before final ruling.

## OBJECTION 2 — First Amendment Retaliation: Dismissal Should Be Without Prejudice / With Leave to Amend

The R&R dismisses this claim solely because you didn't plead **comparator evidence** under Nieves/Grisham (i.e., specific facts showing similarly-situated non-protected-speech individuals weren't arrested/towed the same way). That's a **pleading deficiency**, not a merits failure — the R&R doesn't say such comparators don't exist, only that you didn't plead them.

**Objection:** Dismissal on a pure pleading gap should come with leave to amend, particularly for a pro se plaintiff (Fifth Circuit holds pro se plaintiffs to a more lenient standard — the R&R says so itself, quoting Chhim). If you have or can allege facts about Galveston PD's general towing/booking practices for other DWI stops (ride-along data, other public arrest reports, or even just alleging "on information and belief" that PD does not routinely impound vehicles when willing custodians are present), that should go in an amended pleading rather than a final dismissal.

**Ask:** Modify the recommendation to dismiss this claim without prejudice and with leave to replead comparator allegations, rather than a final dismissal with prejudice.

## OBJECTION 3 — Preserve the Osteen Perjury Finding for the Record (lower priority, but don't skip it)

The R&R affirmatively finds: "the record shows that Osteen lied to Judge Tollison." That finding survives even though the malicious-prosecution claim built on it fails for other reasons (independent probable cause, no malice element). Don't let this fact get buried — it matters for:
- Any future Brady/impeachment argument in the criminal case
- A civil conspiracy or state-law fraud theory if state claims get refiled in state court
- The overall narrative for appeal if this case goes up

**Objection (narrow):** To the extent the R&R's probable-cause analysis on the blood-warrant/Franks claim treats Osteen's proven-false statements as immaterial "because there was other probable cause anyway," that materiality conclusion deserves de novo review — Franks entitles you to a hearing where materiality is tested against the *totality* including how the false statements colored the rest of the affidavit, not just whether independently-recited facts alone would theoretically suffice. Ask, at minimum, for a Franks hearing rather than resolution on the pleadings.
