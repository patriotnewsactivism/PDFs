## OBJECTION 1 — Unreasonable Seizure: the Constitutional Violation Was Found; Qualified Immunity Was Granted Only Because the Clearly-Established Prong Was Never Briefed (strongest ground)

**What the R&R actually holds (get this right — it's better for you than a flat loss):** The magistrate **found Reardon stated a real constitutional violation** — the vehicle impoundment was unreasonable, the community-caretaking exception (*South Dakota v. Opperman*, 428 U.S. 364 (1976)) doesn't apply because there's no evidence the vehicle was disabled, illegally parked, blocking traffic, or had expired registration, and willing private custodians were present. This is not a case where the court doubted your Fourth Amendment claim — that portion is a real win.

The claim dies ONLY at prong two of qualified immunity — "clearly established law" — and the R&R is explicit about why: **"Reardon fails to even address Doraty's and Osteen's assertion of qualified immunity as to the unreasonable seizure claim, much less point the court to case law suggesting that the right... was a clearly-established right at the time that Reardon's vehicle was impounded."** In other words, this was decided by **default/forfeiture in the underlying pro se pleading**, not by the district judge weighing the actual state of the law. The objections stage is your first real chance to brief this.

**Objection:** Present the clearly-established-law argument that was never briefed below.
1. The controlling framework — Opperman's caretaking-rationale limit — was clearly established Fifth Circuit and Supreme Court law well before 2023, reinforced by *Colorado v. Bertine*, 479 U.S. 367 (1987) and *Florida v. Wells*, 495 U.S. 1 (1990) (impoundment/inventory searches require a genuine caretaking rationale under standardized criteria, not officer discretion untethered to any of the recognized justifications).
2. **Do not rely on Degenhardt as supporting authority** — the magistrate already cited it (757) as a case where similarly-situated officers ALSO received qualified immunity, which cuts against, not for, this argument. If Degenhardt is distinguishable on its facts (e.g., different reasons for impoundment, different comparator posture), say so specifically; otherwise leave it alone rather than citing it.
3. Lead instead with ***Hope v. Pelzer*, 536 U.S. 730 (2002)** — officials can be on notice their conduct is unconstitutional even without a case squarely on point, when the conduct is *obviously* unlawful. Towing a citizen's own, undamaged, legally-driveable vehicle over his express objection, with willing custodians standing right there and no caretaking rationale applicable (a fact the magistrate himself already found), is exactly the kind of obvious case Hope addresses. **The R&R never engaged with Hope v. Pelzer or the "obviousness" doctrine at all** — this is genuinely new legal argument for the district judge, not a rehash.
4. Alternative/fallback ask: because this was effectively resolved without adversarial briefing on the dispositive legal question, request that the court, in its discretion, either reject the qualified-immunity recommendation on the *Hope* obviousness theory, or at minimum permit supplemental briefing on that narrow legal question before final ruling — a pro se litigant should get one real chance to brief the point that decided his claim.

**Ask:** Reject the R&R's qualified immunity recommendation on the seizure claim under the Hope v. Pelzer obviousness doctrine; alternatively, request supplemental briefing on that narrow question before final ruling.

## OBJECTION 2 — First Amendment Retaliation: Dismissal Should Be Without Prejudice / With Leave to Amend to Plead Comparator Evidence

The R&R dismisses this claim because Reardon "failed to plead comparator evidence" — i.e., facts showing that similarly-situated people not engaged in protected speech (auditing/journalism) would not also have been stopped/arrested for the same driving conduct (failure to maintain a lane, watery eyes, refusing a field sobriety test). This is a pleading-specificity problem, not a merits problem, and pro se pleadings get liberal construction.

**Objection:** Ask that this dismissal be without prejudice and with leave to replead, specifically to add comparator allegations — e.g., public arrest/booking data for other DWI stops in Galveston, any pattern showing PD does not routinely impound vehicles when willing custodians are present, or specific facts (even alleged "on information and belief") about how other similarly-stopped drivers were treated. This is exactly the kind of curable gap Rule 15's liberal-amendment policy is meant to address, especially for a pro se, indigent, self-representing litigant.

**Ask:** Modify the recommendation to dismiss this claim without prejudice and with leave to replead comparator allegations.

## OBJECTION 3 — Malicious Prosecution / Unlawful Search: Preserve the Osteen False-Statement Findings for the Record, and Push Back on the Franks Materiality Ruling

Two separate, real findings survive even though both claims were dismissed on other grounds — don't let either get buried:
- The R&R states outright: **"the record shows that Osteen lied to Judge Tollison. That is disturbing."** (this concerns Osteen's conduct as it relates to the Mississippi revocation proceeding, separate from the Dkt.79 concession below — both are independent false-statement threads and should be cited separately, not conflated.)
- **Defendants' own June 10, 2026 Reply brief, Dkt. 79 ¶ 14** (filed by Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP), concedes Osteen "stated on camera he could not smell alcohol" — directly contradicting the blood-warrant affidavit's claim of a "moderate odor of alcohol." Confirmed via CourtListener docket 70675448 as being in THIS case.

**On the unlawful-search/Franks claim itself:** the R&R does NOT dismiss this on a technicality — it engages the materiality question directly and concludes that even setting aside Osteen's disputed statements (dilated vs. pinpoint pupils; smelled alcohol from Reardon's breath vs. only from the car), independent facts (failure to maintain a single lane, watery eyes, refused field sobriety test) still supported probable cause for the blood warrant. This is a real, reasoned holding — a weaker target than Objection 1. **Objection (narrow, don't overstate):** *Franks* entitles a challenger to an evidentiary hearing when a substantial preliminary showing of deliberate falsity is made; resolving materiality on the pleadings, without a hearing, denies Reardon the chance to test how far the false statements colored the *totality* of Osteen's affidavit (e.g., whether the "watery eyes/refused FST" facts were themselves relayed by Osteen or corroborated independently) — ask, at minimum, for a Franks hearing rather than resolution on the pleadings, while being upfront that the R&R's independent-probable-cause reasoning is substantive, not just procedural, so this is a preservation-for-appeal objection more than a likely reversal.

**Ask:** Preserve both Osteen false-statement findings for the record (future Brady/impeachment value, appeal record); request a Franks hearing on the materiality question rather than resolution on the pleadings.

## OBJECTION 4 — Excessive Force: Dismissal Should Be Without Prejudice (pleading-specificity problem, not a merits problem)

The R&R dismisses this claim because Reardon "does not specify the nature of his physical injury or psychological harm" beyond conclusory labels. Reardon has a **documented pre-existing shoulder injury requiring three prior hospitalizations** — specific facts about how the forced restraint/blood draw aggravated that injury, plus any specific psychological symptoms (not just the label "psychological harm"), would cure this pleading gap.

**Ask:** Dismiss without prejudice with leave to amend to plead specific physical and psychological injury facts.

## OBJECTION 6 — Busby's Personal-Jurisdiction Dismissal (weak objection — flag only if new facts exist)

The R&R's Rule 12(b)(2) analysis is well-reasoned and hard to attack on the current record: it relies on Busby's own affidavit plus a corroborating affidavit from ADA Steven Jubera showing Jubera — not Busby — personally sent the March 25, 2024 letter to Texas. **Only object here if Reardon has specific, non-conclusory facts showing Busby had OTHER direct contacts with Texas** beyond this letter (e.g., a direct call, email, or communication Busby personally sent into Texas) — otherwise this objection is unlikely to succeed and may not be worth the space in a page-limited filing. Do not object just to object.
