## OBJECTION 1 — Unreasonable Seizure: Qualified Immunity Was Wrongly Applied (strongest ground)

The R&R **already finds** the vehicle impoundment was an unreasonable seizure on the merits — no community-caretaking justification, no evidence of expired registration, willing private custodians present. This is a real win baked into the opinion. It only dies because the Magistrate found no case "clearly establishing" the right at the necessary level of specificity.

**⚠️ VERIFIED CORRECTION (checked 2026-07-23):** *Degenhardt v. Bintliff*, 117 F.4th 747 (5th Cir.), was **decided/filed September 24, 2024** — more than 13 months AFTER the August 2023 arrest. Qualified immunity requires the law to have been clearly established **at the time of the conduct**, so Degenhardt cannot be cited as the "clearly established as of August 2023" authority itself. Use it instead as **persuasive confirmation** that the rule was already inherent in pre-existing doctrine, not as the clearly-established source.

**Objection (reframed, now with 3 layers of pre-2023 authority):** The R&R's clearly-established-law analysis is too demanding. The controlling framework was already clearly established well before August 2023:
1. ***South Dakota v. Opperman*, 428 U.S. 364 (1976)** — permits vehicle impoundment/inventory only when justified by a legitimate community-caretaking rationale (protecting the public, protecting the owner's property, or removing a traffic hazard).
2. ***Colorado v. Bertine*, 479 U.S. 367 (1987)** — impoundment/inventory searches are reasonable only when conducted per standardized, reasonable police procedures serving a genuine caretaking purpose, not as a pretext for investigation.
3. ***Florida v. Wells*, 495 U.S. 1 (1990)** — an inventory search (and, by extension, the impoundment decision behind it) unmoored from standardized criteria is unreasonable; unchanneled discretion to impound is exactly what these cases forbid.
None of the recognized caretaking rationales applied here: the vehicle was legally parked/driveable, not evidence of a crime, and willing private custodians were present and offered to take custody on the spot. This Opperman/Bertine/Wells line was clearly established Fifth Circuit and Supreme Court law decades before 2023. *Degenhardt* (2024) simply confirms courts in this circuit have since applied that same pre-existing framework to materially identical facts (unlawful tow + First Amendment retaliation) and found it clearly violated — showing the right was obvious and knowable in 2023, even though Degenhardt itself post-dates the arrest and cannot be cited as the source of "clearly established" law for this case.

Argue in the alternative and with real weight now: *Hope v. Pelzer*, 536 U.S. 730 (2002), holds officials can be on notice their conduct violates clearly established law even without a materially similar case when the conduct is *obviously* unlawful — towing a citizen's own, undamaged, legally-driveable vehicle over his express objection, with willing custodians standing right there, is exactly that kind of obvious case. Given the Degenhardt dating issue, this "obviousness" argument should be treated as co-equal in weight to the Opperman/Bertine/Wells argument above, not a fallback.

**Ask:** Reject the R&R's qualified immunity recommendation on the seizure claim; alternatively, request supplemental briefing specifically on Hope v. Pelzer "obviousness" and the pre-2023 Opperman/Bertine/Wells line before final ruling.

## OBJECTION 2 — First Amendment Retaliation: Dismissal Should Be Without Prejudice / With Leave to Amend

If the R&R recommends dismissal of the First Amendment retaliation claim for insufficient comparator/pattern-and-practice allegations, object that dismissal should be without prejudice. Pro se pleadings get liberal construction, and any missing specificity (e.g., comparator booking/impound data for other DWI stops — ride-along data, other public arrest reports, or even just alleging "on information and belief" that PD does not routinely impound vehicles when willing custodians are present) is exactly the kind of gap that should be cured by amendment rather than a final dismissal.

**Ask:** Modify the recommendation to dismiss this claim without prejudice and with leave to replead comparator allegations, rather than a final dismissal with prejudice.

## OBJECTION 3 — Preserve the Osteen Perjury Finding for the Record (lower priority, but don't skip it)

The R&R affirmatively finds: "the record shows that Osteen lied to Judge Tollison." That finding survives even though the malicious-prosecution claim built on it fails for other reasons (independent probable cause, no malice element). Don't let this fact get buried — it matters for:
- Any future Brady/impeachment argument in the criminal case
- A civil conspiracy or state-law fraud theory if state claims get refiled in state court
- The overall narrative for appeal if this case goes up

**Objection (narrow):** To the extent the R&R's probable-cause analysis on the blood-warrant/Franks claim treats Osteen's proven-false statements as immaterial "because there was other probable cause anyway," that materiality conclusion deserves de novo review — Franks entitles you to a hearing where materiality is tested against the *totality* including how the false statements colored the rest of the affidavit, not just whether independently-recited facts alone would theoretically suffice. Ask, at minimum, for a Franks hearing rather than resolution on the pleadings.
